# GPSL Laws

This document consolidates the experimental law set into four groups.

## 1. Representation and Mode

### Law A — Representation-Induced Mode Shift
Input representation changes reasoning mode. Symbolic and natural-language inputs do not simply differ in cost; they activate different interpretive pathways.

### Law B — Legend as Entropy Collapse
A legend reduces interpretive entropy in symbolic inputs and collapses search space.

### Law C — Format Dominance Trigger
A sufficiently strong symbolic legend can override input format and induce symbolic output even from natural-language prompts.

### Law D — Semantic Non-Neutrality of Natural Language
Natural language is never fully neutral. Even "headerless" natural-language prompts carry domain priors.

### Law F — Architecture-Specific Ontological Defaults
Different architectures assign different initial interpretations to the same symbolic input.

## 2. Attractors and Constraints

### Law G — Legend Is Generative
A sufficiently structured legend can generate symbolic structure without an external cipher.

### Law H — Conditional Symbolization
Spontaneous symbolization does not arise from abstraction alone. It depends on a compatible representational attractor.

### Law I — Formal Attractor Convergence
Under "formal symbolic" prompting, models tend toward predicate-logic-like notation drawn from shared training priors.

### Law J — Symbolic Anchor Requirement
Some architectures require an explicit symbolic anchor. Without it, they may enter bounded but unproductive search.

### Law K — Architecture-Specific Attractor Weight
Competing attractors do not have universal dominance. Their relative strength is architecture-dependent.

### Law M — Scaffold Determines Resolution Pathway
The presence or absence of a representational scaffold changes how contradiction resolves. Without GPSL: contradiction produces negation or collapse (¬A). With GPSL: contradiction produces ascension or transformation (↑). Confirmed via K4 forcing experiment across architectures.

### Law N — Attractor Controllability
Instruction can suppress or redirect dominant attractors, though the degree of control varies by architecture.

### Law O — Free-Symbol Fixed-Point
At temperature 0.0, architecture-specific free-symbol invention converges to a fixed point. DeepSeek R1 14B independently generates ⊗⇄Ω as its preferred notation on every fresh instance. This is a deterministic symbolic fixed-point, not random invention.

## 3. Failure and Boundaries

### Law E — Semantic Interference Deadlock
Partial overlap between natural-language semantics and symbolic semantics can induce non-terminating translation loops.

### Law L — Misclassification Premium
When symbolic input is misclassified as encoded language rather than logical structure, search cost can increase dramatically. Minimal reclassification can sharply reduce cost.

### Law P — Constraint Satisfaction Loop
A sufficiently internalized grammar may enter bounded recursive search when the specification contains an unresolved ambiguity.

### Law Q — Minimal Permission Resolution
A small, targeted semantic relaxation can collapse a blocked constraint loop and restore generative progress.

## 4. Dynamics and Stability

### Law R — Contextual Internalization
In warm context, a symbolic system transitions from an interpreted object to an internalized grammar, enabling generative extension, rule enforcement, and structural discovery. The depth of internalization is temperature-dependent: lower temperature produces substrate-mode internalization (grammar as reasoning environment); higher temperature produces tool-mode internalization (grammar as construction framework).

### Law T — Constrained Evolution
GPSL should not be freely extended by design, but it can evolve through recurrent, pressure-tested emergence.

### Law U — Dynamic Failure Boundary
Failure boundaries are not fixed. They shift with architecture, context, instruction, and temperature.

### Law V — Relational Stability
Operator stability is relational, not intrinsic. It depends on interaction between operator and condition.

### Law W — Probabilistic Stability
Stability is not binary. Operators have stability distributions across the explored state space.

### Law X — Trajectory-Dependent Stability
Stability depends on the path through the system, not just on static context.

### Law Y — Trajectory–Field Coupling
Trajectories are shaped by the stability field, and in turn reshape that field. GPSL is therefore best modeled as a coupled dynamical system.
